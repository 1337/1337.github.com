Every year you stay in school increases your future salary by 8%

… At least that’s what I saw on Youtube.

But, every year you stay in school is also a year you don’t work. So how would you know how many years to stay in school, at least in a vacuum?

If your meaningful life starts from age 18 and ends at age 65, then the government has 48 years to squeeze out of you. And if you quit high school to start work with a $1 salary, and the salary never increases (because you’re in a vacuum, remember), then you end up with:

$$
(66 - 18) \times 1 = 48
$$

Suppose we try again with you entering the workforce after three years of college. This becomes:

$$
(66 - 21) \times (1 + 0.08 \times 3) = 55.8
$$

Hey, that’s more money than a high school graduate. But we also see a formula here:

$$
y = (66 - 18 - x) \times (1 + 0.08x)
= -0.08x^2 + 2.84x + 48
$$

Which is a parabola. Anyway, the differential for the equation is

$$
y’ = -0.16x + 2.84.
$$

So how many years should we study to maximise lifetime salary? We find when y’ is 0:

$$
0 = -0.16x + 2.84
\\0.16x = 2.84
\\
x = 17.75.
$$

Basically, spherical PhDs get the most money when placed in a vacuum.
