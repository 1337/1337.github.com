Nobody Cares About Your Stupid App

As a mobile developer (if I can be called as such), I download competitor apps for business intelligence. This weekend, I downloaded Didi Kuaidi, whose company has exceeded Uber China’s market cap threefold ($25B vs $8B).

With this sort of market cap, they must have hired hundreds of thousands of smart Chinese developers to make a killer Android app, right? As it turns out, any question I ask here can be answered with ‘no.’

First thing after opening the app–which required more than a page of permissions to install–you are greeted with “Do you want to update?”

A stroke of genius from them: since Google Play Services is not that common in China, people there turn on “allow installations from unknown sources”, which allows/forces apps to update themselves like this.

After you update, you get this glorious map of sooty Beijing:


Why the map centers at Beijing, it is simple: the company is headquartered there. Why, then, can a startup 1/1000 of that size not have to have its app simply center its map in Toronto by default, is mind-boggling.
The app also does not try to obtain the user’s location, which is presumably where most trips would start.

I have received many, many complaints about the map (in my app, whichever one you think it is) being slow, and we just need to fix, optimise, and make it pretty. Well, look at this $25B map:

The map is some sort of custom Google Maps (or a very close imitation of it), which highlights the regions in which Didi Kuaidi offers service. I like that. Instead of doing just that, I have decided to disappoint my users after they perform a search. This allows me to generate a “demand heat map” with these failed searches.

There are many cute intro screens when you try to access the “UberPool of China”. I like those too.


Just when you think you are about done looking through this app, and would like to check the ‘Activity Centre’ to see what’s new. And lo, in all its glory:

It is then when I finally realised:
Nobody cares about your stupid app. Nobody cares if it is polished. All it needs to do is work.

How Much Is Too Much for a Phone?

Smartphones are getting more and more powerful, and people are spending more time looking at their phones as ever. So, how much should they be?

Joe, the average consumer, uses his phablet exclusively. He no longer prints documents, types, or uses his desktop for anything. He believes that the price of “a Samsung”, $700, is justified by the fact that it does everything he needs (emails, WhatsApp, Candy Crush).

Joe (the other one), on the other hand, was an English major. Nowadays, he mostly browses Tumblr in the subway on his way to work, flirts with boys for whom he makes coffee, and watches Netflix on his phone when he gets home. He has an iPhone 6s.

Joe (yet another one), his customer, uses his iPhone 6s Plus almost exclusively for Instagramming his lattes. He paid some $1000 for his iPhone on contract, and he loves it.

So, how much is too much for a phone?
The simple answer is, it doesn’t matter. You are reading a blog post that clearly has no purpose.

Easy Autism Test

Content warning: this article discusses nothing in particular.

Self diagnosis of autism is not reliable; we should not be diagnosing ourselves with anything. Self-diagnosis has high false negative and false positive rates, properties not desirable in an experiment.

You should go to a psychologist for such diagnoses. All, though, people are increasingly diagnosed with autism—which is no surprise, considering almost everyone has a disorder of some kind. To diagnose yourself with autism, without the disadvantages of self-diagnosis, single blind tests should be conducted, using participants who do not know that the test is being conducted, and who/m know you relatively well.

Two of the three following methods require a non-reproducible setting, where you are already holding a conversation with your participants. The procedures cannot be reproduced, on par with other modern psychology experimental standards.

Method 1: De Facto Association

Procedure

Hold a conversation with your participant. Without revealing your intention, state that

Since as you know I am autistic, I [do something completely normal, such as drawing].

Results

If the participant thinks that you are autistic, they will not have reacted to such a statement.
On the other hand, should the participant find you a healthy, normal human being, you will be asked why you said that.

Method 2: Proof by Negation

The second method, derived from the million-dollar social experiment, uses two preposterous premises in order to reveal the one that is more ridiculous, depending on setting.

Procedure

Hold a conversation with your participant. Without revealing your intention, state that

Since I am autistic and [the opposite of your sexual orientation], I [do something completely normal, such as drawing].

Results

If the participant thinks that you are autistic, their response will not contain the autism component, such as “I didn’t know you are [straight]’”.
On the other hand, should the participant find you a healthy, normal human being, you will be responded with “What?”.

Method 3: Scientific Method

The third, and perhaps the easiest method, but with by far the highest false negative rates, is by directly stating your hypothesis to your participant:

[Participant’s name], I have autism.

If you do that, you have autism.

Alternatively, just write an article like this one. Then, when you finish writing, you will know for sure that you have autism.

About peerDependencies

Peer dependencies express the relationship between a “plugin package”, such as jquery-lazy, and its host, jquery. If jquery-lazy claims it works with jquery@<2, then installing it will also install a copy of jquery@1.9.

Fixing peerDependencies errors

If you are installing a package, say, cordova-uglify, and see this in npm 2:

It is telling us that cordova-uglify (the package in your package.json) claims to be compatible with uglify-js@2.4.14, ng-annotate@0.15.4, and clean-css@2.2.2, but your project does not install them.

The solution is to add these dependencies to your own package.json, or kindly ask your package maintainer to fix the problem.
