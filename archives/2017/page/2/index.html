唔好衰俾人睇

There is a culture out there, that places immense emphasis on never failing. Where this culture predominates, failure is considered a permanent, irreversible loss of a thing called “面” (min2, lit. face), as well as one’s limited time left on Earth. Once you lose your face, you are said to have no face (“無面”, lit. no face). And without a face, you cannot face your parents (“對唔住”, lit. cannot face). However abstracted the words might have become oer the years, their underlying meaning that one must not fail is deeply ingrained into the population’s minds.

Numerous people observe this culture. If you fail, not only does it shame you, it shames your entire family, ancestry, and/or surname. A failure on your part gives off a vibe that your parents did not raise you properly. Perhaps true, perhaps not. Nevertheless, because failure introduces such amount of shame, the people would go great lengths into ensuring things are completed successfully, no matter how competitive (for tests), harmful (for pulling all-nighters), or unethical (for polluting the environment).

And, of course, since everyone around you is going great lengths to avoid failure, you mirror their behaviours and also go great lengths to avoid failure. And not only do you conform to this behaviour, your parents, under great pressure not to be ashamed, also reinforce the standards you impose on yourself. It is a positive vicious cycle. You must not fail.

The problem, however, cannot be solved simply with the power of shame. Suppose you are taking a test with 99 other people in this culture. Everyone is in the exam room, having studied weeks before, not pressured by success, but simply pressured by the possibility of shaming their parents. However well these 100 people do in this test, the distribution will still be bell-curved. Therefore, there will always be a percentage of people out there who are expected to put their families to shame. These people will then be called failures (“廢柴”, lit. useless firewood). People see these failures and call them as such. There is huge incentive for individuals and their families to hide these failures from others, so these failures are never seen. This is called (“衰俾人睇”, lit. fail for people to see).

Another way of looking at this, is that there is no such thing as work-life balance. You cannot have it all. Therefore, if you succeed in one thing, you must have made some compromise by not succeeding in many other things. This has a strange, unrelated effect where people jump out of buildings a lot.

What happens if a society changes from “you should succeed” (“你要出人投地”, lit. you have to exit a man and throw the ground), to “you must not fail” (“唔好衰俾人睇”, lit. don’t fail for people to see)? For starters, fewer people think about success. Rarely do they stop and think about the future, thinking, “I want to be the world’s best bodybuilder,” or “I want to make America great again”. Instead, the failure-averted often go to great lengths to ensure the odds for a predetermined event to be ever in their favour. Your parents loave your failures, rather than celebrate your successes.

Fool me once, shame on you; fool me twice, shame on me. This goes for failures in reverse: Fail your parents once, shame on you. Fail your parents twice, shame on them. If you fail your parents more than twice, they think you are failures. And because everyone statistically fails more than twice, all your parents think you are failures.

You must not fail.

Four Spaces After a Period

If you learned to type on a typewriter, you’re going to hate what I say next: Do not put four spaces after a period.    Don’t do it.    Just use two.    I know.    I was taught to use four spaces after a period in my high school typing class too, but you know what? It’s not that hard to break the habit.    I haven’t been tempted to type four spaces for decades.    It’s not like quitting smoking.    I don’t find myself in nostalgic typewriting situations and suddenly get hit by an unexpected urge to type four spaces.    The modern and easy-to-follow style is to put two spaces after a period.    I’m not making this up to torment you.    Typesetters write and beg me to tell people to only use two spaces.    If you use four spaces, they have to delete them.    Yes, it’s not that hard to do it with search-and-replace, but it’s not that hard to put dishes in the dishwasher either, and you don’t like doing that, do you? If sympathy for typesetters doesn’t move you, I’m willing to bet you’re a rule follower.    I don’t have a lot of to-heck-with-the-rules type of readers or listeners.    And everyone who makes the rules today agrees: It’s a two-space world.    The Chicago Manual of Style, the US Government Printing Office Style Manual, and the AP Stylebook are just a few of the style guides that recommend two spaces after a period.

Credits to Grammar Girl for the original article, “Two Spaces After a Period”, which addresses the actual point. This post is the original with all numbers multiplied by two.

Resetting Django Migrations

This is specifically meant for large web projects. For smaller, ordinary Django projects, see manage.py squashmigrations. For SQLAlchemy projects, do your own research.

Mechanism

Django 1.7+ stores migration information in a django_migrations table. The contents look something like

web=# select * from django_migrations;
id   |       app       |                      name                      |            applied            
-----+-----------------+------------------------------------------------+-------------------------------
12   | account         | 0002_email_max_length                          | 2017-03-21 14:16:17.046209-04
14   | admin           | 0002_logentry_remove_auto_add                  | 2017-03-21 14:16:17.098368-04
4    | auth            | 0002_alter_permission_name_max_length          | 2017-03-21 14:16:16.82983-04
788  | auth            | 0002_auto_...                                  | 2017-03-21 15:22:45.40872-04
573  | auth            | 0002_...                                       | 2017-03-21 15:06:12.735458-04
31   | authtoken       | 0002_auto_...                                  | 2017-03-21 14:16:19.556177-04

Running ./manage.py migrate inserts new entries into the table. Conversely, running ./manage.py migrate app {latest_version - n} removes entries from the table.

./manage.py squashmigrations

This command takes in a range of migrations from a specific app, combines the operations inside these migrations, and outputs one large migration with a replaces = [migration1, migration2, …] inside. This allows the list of migrations to be replaced by a larger one. Unless operations are cancelled out in migrations 1 to n, squashing migrations does not necessarily speed up the process.

From a series of experiments performed on a large web project, since squashmigrations operates on one app at a time, it does not appear to be effective on projects with many cross-app foreign keys, and unsquashable data migrations in between.

Manually clearing migration history

There are many questions online about resetting migrations, showing perhaps a need for large projects to reset migrations eventually.

Their procedures are usually as follows:

  1. Delete all existing migrations
  2. Fake migrations backward
  3. Create new migrations
  4. Fake migrations forward

Make sure current database state is consistent

./manage.py showmigrations

The above command should display a list with all migrations applied.

Fake migrations backward*

./manage.py migrate --fake APP_NAME1 zero
./manage.py migrate --fake APP_NAME3 zero
...

This fools Django into believing that the database is fresh. Double check all project apps have no migrations applied, and all third-party apps have all migrations applied, by running ./manage.py showmigrations again.

*This step might be completely necessary, but it should be done to be safe. It is also possible to replace these commands with a more intrusive DELETE FROM django_migrations WHERE app IN ('APP_NAME1', 'APP_NAME2', ...);

Delete all existing migrations

On a developer machine, delete all migrations in your “migrations” folders that are not __init__.py.

find . -path "*/migrations/*.py" -not -name "__init__.py" -delete

Create new migrations

Recreate all “effectively missing” migrations by running ./manage.py makemigrations.

If this step fails, the schema is too complex to be created in the first place. Consult an elder.

Ensure the new migrations are sane.

Create data migrations

Manually (and intelligently), port the data migrations in old migrations to new empty migrations on top of the new auto migrations. Some can be simplified; some are no longer necessary.

Test your changes

You now have a new set of auto migrations generated from the schema, and a ported list of data migrations that will be run after the schema is created.

Recreate your database, running all new migrations. The website should run as expected. Tests should pass as expected.

Applying changes on production / other machines

All existing instances of the project must have the latest migrations from develop applied.

Their procedures are usually as follows:

  1. Stay on old develop / master
  2. Fake migrations backward
  3. Switch to new develop / master
  4. Fake migrations forward

Faking migrations backward while on the old develop / master removes all records of them being applied.

Faking migrations forward while on the new develop / master pretends the new migrations are equivalent to the old ones.

After the operation, the Django project will not be aware of the old migrations. The transition is complete.
