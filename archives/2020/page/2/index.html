She lived as a man & a woman -- here's what she learned

Paula Stone Williams was a man and now a transgender woman. By living life on both sides, she gets to see the world… at least from more perspectives than most of us. Other trans people speak out, too. Let’s see what they have to say, before and after changing gender.

Life as a man, pros

  • People respect you.
  • People take (you, and what you talk about) seriously. This eliminates a man’s need to doubt himself.
  • People don’t assume you are stupid.
  • Men typically aren’t mean to other men.
  • Men don’t need to apologise for being right.
  • Men don’t have periods. They also never hurt due to pregnancy and childbirth.
  • People rarely spike your drinks. Although male rape does exist, it is reported much less frequently.
  • Women don’t (sexually) harass you or, to a lesser extent, your appearance.
  • It is not scary to walk alone at night.
  • Typically, no makeup is required to appear normal.
  • A man’s attractiveness does not drop off sharply at age 30.
  • It is much easier for you to pursue a leadership role if you are a man.
  • The vast majority of organised religions don’t oppress men.

The little things:

  • Jeans pockets can actually hold things.
  • Things never fall out of any pocket.
  • Male skin is thicker and more protective.
  • Clothing sizing numbers actually mean something.

Life as a woman, pros

  • It is okay to be short (being fat is another thing, but at least you can control it).
  • Haircuts are required less often.
  • Women live longer.
  • Women can choose to be fabulous.
  • When it comes to dating, you get plenty of choices.
  • Women are far less lonely in this world, and share their feelings and affection.
  • A woman’s value is not derived from her ability to provide.
  • A woman’s occupation is cleaner on average, mainly skewed by some men working sewage, mining, construction, and other forms of hard labour.
  • Less is expected of women: the world is not on their shoulders. Women do not need (at least seen by society) to do great things, and to achieve great heights in their careers.
  • It is socially acceptable to shop as much as you want and not care about your spending habits.
  • Women are free to be emotional—even weak—if so desired.
  • Women can have children, if that’s what you want.
  • Getting lighter sentences for crimes like rape is among the social, political, and legal double standards that prefer women, such as custody.
  • In most countries, women are never conscripted for war.

/u/Zenla8282 put it nicely: I think both men and women have it harder and easier in different ways, but usually when life gets tough, most of us don’t think “wow my life is so hard because I’m a woman/man”.

Things to buy when everything is OK

2020 is surely an excellent year for everyone (for better or for worse). Viruses, stock market crashes, recessions, and now protests are breaking out. There were so many things I wish I did ahead of time, including preparing myself appropriately. By no means stockpile useless items like toilet paper, here are some things you can buy before life hits you hard.

Survival packs

It is really, really hard to order survival kits when Amazon is backlogged and takes a month to ship anything. They also get stupid expensive because everybody wants one, so buy these things way ahead of time. You don’t need to use it. Unless you have food inside, survival kits don’t expire.

Car Repairs

In the case of mass protests and viral outbreaks, you may need to escape your residence, so you really don’t want to have to book an appointment for an oil change before you can hit the road.

Realistically speaking, since you can store gasoline for at most six months (the octane rating gets wacky), you cannot go far by car even if you want to.

Gold and gold stocks

Gold is usually cheaper to buy when fiat currencies aren’t crashing and burning from indefinite QE and riots and trade wars. Since gold is intrinsically worth much more than the dollar (despite also having as much value as society gives it), gold holds value relative to an inflating currency, almost guaranteeing a relative rise in value over time.

But you can’t buy gold when everyone else is panic-buying gold as well.

Buying gold stocks allows you to earn dividends when gold depreciates, which is most of the time during economic growth.

Gun company stocks

When protests break out, especially in the US, people rush to buy guns. You don’t have to agree with a gun company to profit from it. In fact, by buying gun company stocks low and selling high, you potentially hurt the company (albeit not by much) by costing it dividends when it needed money the most.

In June 2020, “American Outdoor Brands Corporation” aka Smith & Wesson rose 10% on a single day.

Solar panels

I have always wanted some. Depending on which ones you get, they are going to be useful even after uncertain times.

Every year you stay in school increases your future salary by 8%

… At least that’s what I saw on Youtube.

But, every year you stay in school is also a year you don’t work. So how would you know how many years to stay in school, at least in a vacuum?

If your meaningful life starts from age 18 and ends at age 65, then the government has 48 years to squeeze out of you. And if you quit high school to start work with a $1 salary, and the salary never increases (because you’re in a vacuum, remember), then you end up with:

$$
(66 - 18) \times 1 = 48
$$

Suppose we try this again with you entering the workforce after three years of college. This becomes:

$$
(66 - 21) \times (1 + 0.08 \times 3) = 55.8
$$

Hey, that’s more money than a high school graduate. But we also see a formula here:

$$
y = (66 - 18 - x) \times (1 + 0.08x)
= -0.08x^2 + 2.84x + 48
$$

Which is a parabola. Anyway, the differential for the equation is

$$
y’ = -0.16x + 2.84.
$$

So how many years should we study to maximise lifetime salary? We find when y’ is 0:

$$
0 = -0.16x + 2.84
\\0.16x = 2.84
\\
x = 17.75.
$$

Basically, spherical PhDs get the most money when placed in a vacuum.

What is RSA?

Part of the KA series.

Prime numbers

Prime numbers are… not composite numbers. Prime numbers only have two factors: 1, and itself. Prime numbers can be divided by no other number.

Composite numbers can always be broken down to smaller primes.
So… every number can be expressed by a number of smaller primes. Every number has one, and only one, prime factorisation; no two numbers share the same prime factorisation (because you can’t multiply one set of numbers and get two different numbers back.)

Modular arithmetic

Given a modulus…

46 mod 12 = 10

We say 46 mod 12 is congruent to 10.

A prime modulus (say 17), combined with a primitive root (say 3, called a generator), when in the form

(3^n) mod 17 = ...

And it has an interesting property, where if you have enough ns, the solution distributes uniformly from 0 to 16.

However, the reverse question, called the discrete logarithm problem, is hard. The question is, given:

(3^?) mod 17 = 12

Find ?.

This is very hard—especially if the modulus (17) is a huge number—which makes it a good one-way function.

Diffie-Hellman key exchange

First, two parties (Alice, A, and Bob, B) agree on a prime modulus and a generator, in the form g mod p. Let’s pick g = 3 and p = 17.
Then, A raises her message by a private random number, say 15, i.e.

g^15 mod p = 3^15 mod 17 = 6

A sends 6 publicly to B.

B receives 6, selects his own private random number, say 13, and calculates his own g mod p

g^13 mod p = 3^13 mod 17 = 12

B sends 12 back to A.

Shared secret

A performs yet another g mod p, with g being B’s public message, raised to her private number:

g^15 mod p = 12^15 mod 17 = 10

10 being the shared secret.

B performs yet another g mod p, with g being A’s public message, raised to his private number:

g^13 mod p = 6^13 mod 17 = 10

Notice how the two parties got the same number! They actually did the same calculation (it doesn’t really matter):

3^(13 * 15) mod 17 = 3^(15 * 13) mod 17 = 10

#Euler’s totient function

Euler proposed a Phi function:

φ(n)

φ(n) looks at integers from 1 to n, and returns however many numbers that n does not share a factor with. For example, φ(8) would mean 1, (shares a factor 2) 3, (shares a factor 2) 5, (shares a factor 2), 7, (shares a factor 2), which is 4.

φ(n) is easy to do for prime numbers. φ(n) is just n-1 for prime numbers because it doesn’t share a common factor with any integer less than it:

φ(p) = p - 1

Multiplicative phi

φ(a * b) = φ(a) * φ(b).

In other words, if n were a product of two primes, then

φ(n) = φ(p1 * p2) = φ(p1) * φ(p2) = (p1 -1) * (p2 -1).

This will be useful in the next chapter.

Euler’s Theorem

Now that we have a φ(n), and we know it is easy to calculate φ(n) if n is a product of two primes, p1 and p2, then finding φ(n) is easy (see above). We will also need to know the Euler’s Theorem(s):

(m ^ φ(n)) = 1 mod n

(as long as m and n do not share a common factor, which is the case for primes.)

Let’s go through an example with m=5 and n=8:

(m ^ φ(n)) = 1 mod n
(5 ^ φ(8)) = 1 mod 8
   (5 ^ 4) = 1 mod 8
       625 = 1 mod 8

Then we introduce two facts of life,

  1. 1^k = 1 for any k
  2. 1*m = m for any m

Modify the initial equation with the added magic

     (m ^ φ(n)) = 1 mod n
 m * (m ^ φ(n)) = m mod n
m * (m ^ kφ(n)) = m mod n
m ^ (kφ(n) + 1) = m mod n  # Because m * m = m^2

Recall (if you watched the original KA videos) that

m ^ e*d = m mod n

(d being A’s private key) mapping e*d to kφ(n) + 1.

So, what’s RSA encryption?

The GCHQ developed this encryption, but it was classified when Rivest–Shamir–Adleman published their paper, which was researched independently, so now RSA takes the cake.

Simplified RSA in action

Alice generates two primes, two around the same size but p1 is smaller than p2.

p1 = 53
p2 = 59
 n = p1 * p2 = 53 * 59 = 3127

Then she calculates her φ(n)

φ(n) = φ(3127) = 3016

Next, she picks some small public exponent, say e = 3, as long as e is an odd number, and e does not share a factor with φ(n).

Then she calculates her private key with one of the equations above (and, for some reason, k = 2[^3]:

e*d = kφ(n) + 1
  d = (kφ(n) + 1) / e
  d = (2 * 3016 + 1) / 3
  d = 2011

Next, she selects n (3127) and e (3), which together makes up her public key.

Bob receives n (3127) and e (3), then encrypts his message (for some reason, m = 89) with:

    m ^ e mod n = c
89 ^ 3 mod 3127 = 1394

Bob sends the encrypted message c (1394) back to Alice.

Alice takes her d (2011), which only she knows, and raises Bob’s secret message (1394) by her d, netting this equation:

c ^ d = m mod n

Which is secure, because we know d maps to (kφ(n) + 1), which is a one-way function.

[^3]: “Remember that there is no single value for k, in fact there are an infinite number of k’s that would work” - brit cruise
