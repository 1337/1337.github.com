Ubermiling: Extreme Guide to Increase Your Miles per Gallon

There is a list of tips from hypermilers, each one more ridiculous than the last. The list gets longer every day (108 at this point).
But, hey, why stop there?

  1. Replace your windows with thinner glass
    The only point of windows is to reduce drag. Replace them with plastic windows, which are significantly lighter. Why not? Everyone is doing it.
  2. Tape the gaps between the doors you don’t use
    Tape your doors shut, so air does not get trapped between the door and the gaps. Top gear did it in Ukraine once, obtaining insane MPG figures.
  3. Strip your paint off, and replace with golf balls
    The average car carries “200 pounds” of paint (whichever pound it is). That’s a lot! Strip everything off, and replace the surface with the golf ball pattern, which reduces drag
  4. Don’t brake when turning
    Want to turn right? Turn right! You have suspensions, right? Use them! Who cares about pesky maintenance costs when you can save pennies on fuel?
  5. Drive only downhill, and push your car uphill (#60)
    Driving uphill costs more fuel than driving downhill. The obvious solution is to drive exclusively downhill. And only in strong tailwinds.
    This is actually an extension of “Push it - 2” on the original list.
    Since you are going downhill all the time, technically you will not be needing an engine or transmission to drive your car. They are heavy components that are only there to weigh you down.
    In order to measure fuel economy, you will still need a motor in your car, so carry one of those with you.
  6. Dehydrate yourself
    You are made of 70% water. Removing all water from yourself yields more than a few pennies of savings.
  7. Replace your tires with bicycle tires (#106)
    Higher pressure tires give you more fuel savings. Narrower tires are also more economic. What do? Bicycle tires, obviously!
    Don’t forget to remove as many spokes as possible, as well!
  8. Replace your battery
    The average lead-acid car battery can weigh kilograms. You can find some CR1025 cells that weigh 0.7 grams.
  9. Wait for the snow plow (#80)
    When the snow plow arrives, don’t forget to hitch a free ride in front of it.
  10. Strip naked (#93)
    “If you regularly commute to work in hot weather, you might find that (stripping) mean you can run the air conditioner a little less.”
  11. Only drive during the day
    What do you need to drive during the night? Lights. What do you need to power the lights? Gasoline. Smart up, people!
    Don’t forget to disconnect your headlights so the daylight running lights don’t come on (#34).
  12. Drive at either 50km/h or 90km/h (#99)
    The two are the only correct answers.
  13. Remove all airbags
    Did you know that 99% of all airbags manufactured end up in the landfills unused? Chances are, you have not used yours, either. Drop the weight. Porsche does this for some of their cars.
  14. Lower your ride
    The lower your car is, the less drag it creates from the under panels (which I assume yours are a perfectly-flat sheet of metal).
  15. Reduce the size of your side mirrors
    Smaller, aftermarket mirrors reduce the surface area of your car, lowering aerodynamic resistance.
  16. Coat your road surface with oil
    We all know road surfaces are the largest source of friction when it comes to cars travelling in a spherical vacuum. The fuel-saving solution to that is to coat your daily commute with lubricants that help maintain your speed.
  17. Block your grille
    If you block your grille, that means there’s no air turbulence inside. Speed speed speed!
  18. Remove half of your lugnuts
    If you have four nuts on each wheel, take two off from each. You will want to keep the wheels balanced, obviously, for fuel economy reasons, so if your wheels are imbalanced in the first place, remove lug nuts, as necessary, from whichever side the wheel is heavier.
    Remember you are using bicycle tires, so you can find lighter rims that fit them.
  19. Drive in a vacuum
    You cannot drive in space, but if you could, you will encounter far less aerodynamic drag than if you were driving on Earth.
    The solution is to find a downhill vacuum on Earth to drive in, with the exception being in tailwinds stronger than your intended speed of 50km/h.
  20. Drive only in New Hampshire
    New Hampshire has no law around seat belts, which I assume you have already removed from your vehicle, along with your stereo, speakers, air conditioning unit, dashboard, power windows, every seat apart from yours, and floor board for maximum weight reduction.
    Driving in New Hampshire, therefore, should make you the best ubermiler.
  21. Don’t stop at border crossings
    Braking is just wasted energy. As you approach the border crossing, simply ease your throttle until the police bring you to a complete stop.

In summary, your sweet ride will have/be:

  • An electric motor, directly connected to a hearing aid battery
  • You, in the form of a jerky
  • Plastic windows, bicycle tires, and basically nothing else
  • In New Hampshire
  • Atop a frictionless oily road
  • Accelerating downhill in a vacuum at (at most) 9.81ms-2, or
  • Travelling the wind, at 90km/h. Remember to coast with your motor off

Spending exactly no fuel, you will have beaten the world record by a long shot. Given enough time, you might even beat the fuel economy of Voyager 2!

How to Make Mistakes in Python

tl;dr No wonder the book is free.

O’Reilly offered us this free eBook titled “How to make Mistakes in Python.” I am not sure if it was very good. Here are the key points.

Setup

  • Not using pip
  • Using the default REPL (Author recommended Jupyter but iPython is obviously superior)

Silly things

  • Not returning a value
  • Typos
  • Using def when you should have used class (which is 100% valid syntax)

Style

  • Using notationTheHungarian
  • Violating pep8
  • Stupid names like x and c or class TestImagineAClassNameThatExceeds80Characters
  • Complicated lambdas
  • Long ass-comprehensions

Structure

  • “Pathological if/elif blocks”, ie. Complicated flow control (cyclomatic complexity)
  • Useless getters and setters
  • Loads of decorators
  • Breaking the Law of Demeter, ie. Accessing attributes of arguments
  • Too many “__private” attributes
  • God objects and God methods
  • Things with global state

Surprises

  • Importing everything
  • Catching everything
  • Reinventing the standard library
  • Mutable argument defaults
  • Not using generators and wasting memory
  • Expecting tearDown is run in tests
  • Not logging
  • Not testing

These are really, really obvious mistakes that the apprentice developer would make, perhaps exclusively before they get a job, or before they finish high school. I hope they never happen to you.

Travel Blog: Tobermory

For absolutely no reason whatsoever, I got myself a holiday to Tobermory, a place that my colleague said was a nice place to visit. She was right.

Just a few hours’ drive from wherever you think I live, and yet a few hours really isn’t the kind of time you would normally plan on wasting, I went on a drive there, again, for absolutely no reason whatsoever, to where the province’s national marine park lies.

Day one involved ending up at Owen Sound, a town that tries to be a city, and subsequently travelling to Tobermory, where all the promises come true. Well, as it turned out, Owen Sound was a bit of a ghost town… at least for the parts it called downtown.

Ordered a takeout sushi bento for absolutely no reason whatsoever. For whatever reason, I always get Japanese food whenever I feel like it. Yummy Yummy, the place, half empty at the time, interestingly, was not bad. The salmon was dry and tasteless, but the rest of the bento box was great.

The Indian Falls was nearby, so I went there. After having just been to Elora, this manmade waterfall, (if it wasn’t manmade, it sure looks like it was.) despite its numerous defencive strategies to prevent the average hiker from reaching there (misleading paths), was underwhelming. I left soon afterwards.

The journey continued on with a drive to Cape Croker, apparently a native American stronghold, and the host of a lighthouse, creatively called the Cape Croker Lighthouse. The path turned from tarmac to gravel, but my car journeyed on. When I arrived, it was covered with pride. And nearby cluster flies.

No time to waste. Time to actually go to Tobermory. I buckled up (hehe) and cruise controlled all the way to the motel, the place that claimed to be a motel, but was actually much nicer. Friendly man there explained that there was a router, but there was effectively no Wi-Fi inside my room. Nice. He also brought forth a helpful map… in retrospect, not that helpful, because “downtown” is literally that one street (Bay St.), and the big/little tubs (like bays or lagoons).

Well, guy said the Big Tub Lighthouse was 45 minutes of walking, but only 2 minutes of drive away. Challenge Accepted. The drive ended up to be more like four minutes, but it was fast nevertheless. The lighthouse was one of the most complete, best preserved lighthouses on this trip.

Had dinner at the creatively-named “The Fish and Chip Place”. It advertised Georgian Bay Whitefish, which, of course, I had to try. It tasted rather gamy. That one museum I visited later tomorrow taught me why.

Wait, it was 7pm and there was one more hour left until sunset. What would Jesus do? He would go to The Grotto! Driving there, the camp guard said the national park was actually closed, no one was here to handle the ticketing, and that if I were to go see The Grotto, I would be there after sunset, and it would be impossible to see anything.

Challenge Accepted, as usual.

And challenge completed.

The view was breathtaking. The rocks, the sun, the open bay.

But I knew I would be in trouble if I do not exit the national park by the time the sun actually sets, so I rushed back to my car.

The morning after, I purchased a light roast coffee from what they call The Coffee Shop. Next, I visited the Visitors Centre, where I should have gone the night before. It was a great hike, full of interesting fungi, and to the watchtower, where I saw the largest unbroken stretch of forest in Southern Ontario.

Having booked a cruise to the Flowerpot Island, part of the Fathom Five National Marine Park, I took a glass bottom ship around the bay, the shipwreck, and the lighthouse I had visited the night before.

The flowerpot island featured three fantastic hikes, two flowerpots, and an amazing Parks Canada staff called Sheila (I think). She was just so enthusiastic about her work, despite all that it was being talking about the island, and ahanding out plastic bags all day long. She left a distinct memory in my mind. She had a very bright smile. She told me all about the island, including the plants and exotic orchids that were not flowering at the time. She loved her work. She loved Georgian Bay. She loved Canada.

I took place in two of the hikes. The first hike, which everyone goes on and back out of, was good. At the end was a lighthouse, or, more accurately, a lightstation, which was underwhelming compared to the sights from the viewing platform. The second hike, towards Marl Bed, a place so hidden it is not even visible on Google Maps, was a bit more difficult. With 50 minutes left to spare before the return ship arrives, I ventured on.

Jesus does provide.

The express ship back was nothing short of exciting; thank the cruise booking staff for “accidentally” booking this ship for me, so that I had two hours of hiking time. And thank her for booking the fast ship back, for it was a unique(ly exciting) experience on a choppy sea.

On my way back, I visited Inglis Falls, the place I had missed completely on my way to Owen Sound. It looked a lot better than Indian Falls. Of course, after having been to Banff, nothing really quite surprised me.

All this, I was incredibly lucky in visiting the area while taking a weekday off. Had I not done that, it would have been packed with people everywhere I go. A weekday trip made the town exquisite, pleasant, and photogenic as well.

The overall fuel consumption was 6.05 L/100km. It will probably never hit that low again.
