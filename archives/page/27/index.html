Writing Annoying Blog Post Titles

I am a contributor of Downworthy, a Chrome extension that removes annoying words from online headings and bodies, sometimes known as linkbaits or clickbaits. Since it is disappointing to click on an interesting-sounding link, for example:

How I earned a million dollars overnight

Just to be followed by a slow page, megabytes of useless JavaScript and images, and a ton of ads:

I bought the lottery and won. Here are five paragraphs about the process, how much I spent on the ticket, how I felt, and how it affects you and me. EXCLUSIVE: 35 SHOCKING SELFIES I TOOK WHEN I COLLECTED MAH MONEY

Naturally, to avoid future incidences of clicking on these mid-grade links, we must first analyse the components of a linkbait title, and how we can neutralise such titles so that normal titles, which may well contain more interesting stories, can get their fair share of our clicks.

Anatomy of a stupid linkbait title

Let’s first look at the front page of Buzzfeed, one of the most pathetic websites on the planet.

You will find their titles fit in one or more of these three categories.

Lists, particularly lists that somehow you can relate:

  • 17 Things To Do When You Are Bored Out Of Your Mind
  • 5 Brilliant BuzzFeed Newsletters That Will Always Brighten Your Day
  • 19 Superfood Combinations That Will Make You Feel Like A Champion
  • 21 Embarrassing Things Every Muggle Secretly Does
  • 19 Signs You’re Definitely Eric Forman From “That ’70s Show”

Titles beginning with Who/What/When/Where/Why, whether or not they are actual questions:

  • What Type Of Shark Are You?
  • Which Sci-Fi Hero Should You Hook Up With?
  • How One Company Is Building An Internet Connection Through The Arctic Thanks To Climate Change
  • What It’s Like To Go Blind
  • What The World Was Like In 1974 When Richard Nixon Resigned

Exaggeration:

  • This Fact About Killer Whales Will Blow Your Mind
  • The Most Fab And Drab Celebrity Outfits Of The Week
  • 24 Pictures That Prove That Life Hacks Have Gone Way Too Far
  • This Timelapse Video Of Pyongyang, North Korea Is Beautiful And Intriguing (it wasn’t)

Reading comprehension:

  • Most viral articles by Buzzfeed have vocabulary aimed at 10-year-olds.

Takeaways

There are only two things we have learned from this exercise:

  • Annoying titles overpromise
  • Annoying articles underdeliver

To avoid wasting time on these posts (including this one, for some), I suggest installing the downworthy plugin!

Investment 001

Suppose you are in your twenties to mid fourties, have some money to play with, and have no kids. (If you happen to be married, congratulations! The society wants your money and they want you dead) What do you do with your money? Under heavy paternal influence, I spent some of it in the stock market.

My one rule: have money

This is Investment 001, the article aimed at people who have a pile of cash, don’t know what to do with it, and would rather buy a nice, 60-inch smart TV for the living room than putting the money to good use. (If you don’t have the money to invest with, what are you investing?)

My other one rule: know what you are doing

If there’s anything my mother taught me, it is that the key to anything is practice. As a mother, she knew how to do things, and do things right.

Here you are, on this blog, wondering how to get started; it has occurred to you that it is near impossible to just “get started”, or google “how to get started in investing“ because the results are different for everyone. As a son of a mother, the first thing I learned to do was knowing what you are doing before doing what you will do.

Which one should I buy (WOSIB)?

  • If you have more than $10000000 to invest in, consider bribing your local government with lobbyists.
  • If you have more than $1000000 to invest in, consider buying a house near a freshwater lake.
  • If you have less than $100 to invest in, consider buying some weights and protein shakes from Wal-mart for mad gains.
  • If you have any other amount of money, consider buying stocks, bonds, and mutual funds.
  • If you don’t know how much you have, consider buying just mutual funds.

A house, also known as property in this industry, are usually houses that you don’t live in. If you live in a house, it is called a liability (because you are in it, you cannot sell it, and as long as you are in it, it will only wear out.) Stocks are fractions of a company that you can buy. The moment you buy your first stock, you are a shareholder! A very small shareholder. Bonds are like stocks, but instead of companies, you buy the government’s debts. The government “borrows money from you”, agreeing to give you a bit more in return. Mutual funds are a mix of any of the above, managed by one or more managers, who typically know(s) better.

When you buy which depends on the state of the market. For example, if a recession is going on, stocks are likely dropping in prices, and you should be careful buying or selling them.

  • Consider buying stocks in a bull market: a market where people are starting to gain confidence in investing.
  • Never buy stocks in a bear market: a market whose general prices are falling, and people are being pessimistic.
  • Consider buying mutual funds if you really don’t care, or some houses to rent out if you have a pile of money, and really don’t care.

My other one rule: don’t lose any money

The first thing I learned about investing is that you shouldn’t be losing any money; this is invariably true. Anything your wife calls “investments”, such as that new dress or that new car, are not investments. They are not investments because they don’t earn you any money (unless you travel with that car to places where you earn money–and even then, this definition is debatable.) Not losing anything money is particularly hard when inflation is factored in, where not investing is a net loss of 3% per month. Therefore, the key to success in investing is to figure out how not to lose any money – a story for another day.

I am just someone you read about on the Internet. Do not believe in everything I say. Make your own decisions.

Making my First App

With Android being all the hype these days, I have decided to make an app.

Having abandoned Java since 2010 (excluding that one bug fix in one of the company’s repositories in 2014), I really had nothing to lose. The syntax, the types, the way projects are built, event handling… I have forgotten all that. This app will be a fresh start to programming something again – something that isn’t run in the browser.

Downloading the SDK

Have you used eclipse? Of course you have. But do you like Eclipse? That’s a much less certain question to answer.

It was actually JetBrains’ release of the first usable SDK that had my interest. When version 0.8 was released, all the screenshots looked like it looked like PyCharm, which is what I use for work and personal projects already. Presumably, with Android Studio, I would be able to add code, edit interfaces, and code like a normal human being, right…?

That is correct!

Unlike Eclipse, the de facto Java IDE from the software giant of Ottawa, JetBrains products are not complete and utter shit; instead, they are designed to get out of the way of the developer. Hell, I don’t even know how to change the editor’s background from white to black in Eclipse.

Creating a new project

Creating a new project is easy enough. Android Studio has a nice wizard that guides you through the process. The wizard creates a project, an activity, and a layout / “content view” inside that activity (which is some kind of abstraction… see later posts.)

Creating the UI

I remember from the Visual Basic 6 days that UIs were extremely easy to make and configure. For instance, if you wanted a number box with a default value, min and max, you would drag one of such element into the window (“form”), enter these values on the right hand Properties pane, and call it a day.

In Android, it is different. To be precise, there is no such thing as doing something intuitively in Java/Android. Here is why:

Finishing the Project

The rest of the build process is very streamlined, however! Android Studio itself includes a way to build a release APK right from its menu. My first app wasn’t qualified for Google Play Store (because it didn’t have a custom icon), but at least the APK was valid and functional.

Creating a Blog

Heard from someone I know (but wish not to disclose his/her identity) that Hexo is a nice framework to use for generating a static blog. Having never done that before, I thought, what the hell!

My experience with Hexo has been very pleasant (or… much better than my previous encounters with node and npm). Here are a few take-aways:

  • Incorrect permissions of the local npm cache (caused by sudo) can and will fail most of the npm- and nodejs-related tasks that you will be running.
  • Inserting images in Hexo (apparently /source is the root path, not / as the config suggests)
  • livereload requires a browser plugin.
  • You need to click on livereload’s button in the browser…
  • EJS, because the theme uses it
  • Stylus, because that’s also what the theme requires; it allows semicolons and brackets to be omitted anywhere you want!
  • Apparently SASS is deprecating its bracket-free syntax
  • How to create referenced links.
  • How to deploy this blog. Booboo! You don’t commit and push – you deploy and push to a draft branch.
  • Deploying blogs to github under a custom domain.

It has been an amazing learning experience! I’m taking a lot of notes as I go. More to come tomorrow.
