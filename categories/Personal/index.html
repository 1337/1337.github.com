How to talk to people

炸 [zhá]

I make my living talking to people. Nobel Prize winners, truck drivers, billionaires, kindergarten teachers, heads of state, plumbers… I talk to people that I like; I talk to people that I don’t like; I talked to some people that I disagree with deeply on a personal level, but I still have a great conversation with them.

Don’t multitask.

Be in that moment. Don’t think about anything else. Remove your conversational filters and talk, and share (aka “give and take”). If you think about your filters, you stop paying attention.

There is no reason to learn how to show you’re paying attention if you are in fact paying attention.

After “that moment”

  • Think about what happened and what was discussed.
  • Assess your body language that you used at the time.
  • Assess your body language that the other people used at the time.

Don’t pontificate. (3.1)

v. To express opinions or judgments in a dogmatic way.

Set your opinions aside. Enter every conversation assuming you have something to learn.

This also means: be a listener.

Use open-ended questions.

Don’t ask questions that can be answered with “yes” or “no”. If you don’t know how to do that, ask questions that start with “who”, “what”, “when”, “where”, or “why”, all of which guarantee an answer that is not “yes” or “no”.

Go with the flow.

Conversation ideas can come and go. You might think of a clever thing to ask two minutes before the person stopped talking, at which point it is no longer appropriate to ask it. Let them come and let them go.

If you don’t know, say that you don’t know.

Don’t claim to be an expert on anything.

Don’t equate your experience with theirs. (3.2)

It is never the same. All experiences are individual. Don’t take the moment to share your experience.

But remember don’t be afraid to be vulnerable. Everyone is flawed in some way. Don’t be afraid to show it.

Try not to repeat yourself.

It’s condescending.

Stay out of the weeds.

No one cares about the details (weeds). Leave them out.

LISTEN. (2.4)

If your mouth is open, you’re not learning. - Claims to be said by a buddha
He who talks more is sooner exhausted. - Lao Tzu

People love to talk about the things that matter to them the most. (2.4) Listen to them. Talk to them about those things. (2.5)
You have to listen with the intent to understand, not just the intent to answer.

Be brief.

Be genuinely interested in other people. Everyone has some hidden, amazing things about them. Keep your mouth shut for as long as possible. (2.1)

Smile. (2.2)

Fake it ‘til you make it.

Call people by their names. (2.3)

People love their own names.

If all else fails

Stick to the weather (excluding “global warming is a hoax”) and your health (excluding topics about vaccines).

Living in an aprtment: pros and cons

My landlord is letting his place go on sale. This also means my eviction is in order (and, given the reason, has a very generous notice required by law). It is time to revisit what it was like to live in an apartment, and what people with similar experiences gained/missed.

Pros

  1. Stairs optional.
  2. Parking is usually underground. With locks.
  3. Gated entry guaranteed.
  4. No one ever steals your packages.
  5. Usually more to see out of windows (unless you rent the basement)
  6. Laundry en suite or in building is almost guaranteed.
  7. There may be other amenities like a fitness room, grocery store, and even a pool. Excellent for people wanting/waiting to die here.
  8. The power will probably never go out. If the power goes out, poor old grannies will be trapped, and they probably won’t let that happen.
  9. No snow. No grass.
  10. Good view out of the window, possibly.
  11. Much better chances for you to sniff someone else’s packets and hack their Wi-Fis.

Cons

  1. Possible noise from upstairs, downstairs, neighbours, pets.
  2. Possible noise from the building itself (if central air).
  3. Your neighbours can be jerks, racists, alcohol/drug addicts, murderers, sex offenders… basically anything except smokers.
  4. Really troublesome to move in and out.
  5. Lifts can be slow, crowded, and awkward to share with someone. They can also break down.
  6. It takes minutes rather than seconds to leave the building. Add more time if driving your car out of that underground parking.
  7. Mailboxes are small, and no one leaves your packages at your door. Because they can’t get to your door. (There are exceptions)
  8. Setting off the smoke alarm is everyone’s problem.
  9. No space to wash your car.
  10. Much higher likelihood of someone sniffing your packets and hacking your Wi-Fis.
  11. Way more diseases (I originally typed this in 2017, but this became extremely relevant in 2020.)
  12. Possibly pests (bed bugs, cockroaches, fleas, rats…) introduced by your lovely neighbours and their pets.

唔好衰俾人睇

There is a culture out there, that places immense emphasis on never failing. Where this culture predominates, failure is considered a permanent, irreversible loss of a thing called “面” (min2, lit. face), as well as one’s limited time left on Earth. Once you lose your face, you are said to have no face (“無面”, lit. no face). And without a face, you cannot face your parents (“對唔住”, lit. cannot face). However abstracted the words might have become over the years, their underlying meaning that one must not fail is deeply ingrained into the population’s minds.

Numerous people observe this culture. If you fail, not only does it shame you, it shames your entire family, ancestry, and/or surname. A failure on your part gives off a vibe that your parents did not raise you properly. Perhaps true, perhaps not. Nevertheless, because failure introduces such amount of shame, the people would go great lengths into ensuring things are completed successfully, no matter how competitive (for tests), harmful (for pulling all-nighters), or unethical (for polluting the environment).

And, of course, since everyone around you is going great lengths to avoid failure, you mirror their behaviours and also go great lengths to avoid failure. And not only do you conform to this behaviour, your parents, under great pressure not to be ashamed, also reinforce the standards you impose on yourself. It is a positive vicious cycle. You must not fail.

The problem, however, cannot be solved simply with the power of shame. Suppose you are taking a test with 99 other people in this culture. Everyone is in the exam room, having studied weeks before, not pressured by success, but simply pressured by the possibility of shaming their parents. However well these 100 people do in this test, the distribution will still be bell-curved. Therefore, there will always be a percentage of people out there who are expected to put their families to shame. These people will then be called failures (“廢柴”, lit. useless firewood). People see these failures and call them as such. There is huge incentive for individuals and their families to hide these failures from others, so these failures are never seen. This is called (“衰俾人睇”, lit. fail for people to see).

Another way of looking at this, is that there is no such thing as work-life balance. You cannot have it all. Therefore, if you succeed in one thing, you must have made some compromise by not succeeding in many other things. This has a strange, unrelated effect where people jump out of buildings a lot.

What happens if a society changes from “you should succeed” (“你要出人投地”, lit. you have to exit a man and throw the ground), to “you must not fail” (“唔好衰俾人睇”, lit. don’t fail for people to see)? For starters, fewer people think about success. Rarely do they stop and think about the future, thinking, “I want to be the world’s best bodybuilder,” or “I want to make America great again”. Instead, the failure-averted often go to great lengths to ensure the odds for a predetermined event to be ever in their favour. Your parents loave your failures, rather than celebrate your successes.

Fool me once, shame on you; fool me twice, shame on me. This goes for failures in reverse: Fail your parents once, shame on you. Fail your parents twice, shame on them. If you fail your parents more than twice, they think you are failures. And because everyone statistically fails more than twice, all your parents think you are failures.

You must not fail.
