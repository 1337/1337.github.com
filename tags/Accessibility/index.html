10 Things Crutches Have Taught Me

(Title ripped directly from this post.)

I went skiing last Christmas, and The Blue Mountains had claimed my knee (some ligaments don’t grow back by themselves, apparently). A group of wonderful friends endured the ordeal with me, an experience I will never forget (medal winners were Jill, Joel, Sultan, and Thomas; special mentions go to family and the government.)

Knee’s useless now. Crutches are in order. Got a pair from the hospital. The last two days were the most physically challenging time of my life.

You don’t have two arms when you have two legs

Want to walk? Use crutches. How are you using the crutches? With both hands. What can you do while both hands are occupied? Nothing. Even the simplest things can be near impossible to do:

  • Carrying a glass of water to another room
  • Carrying a plate of food
  • Carrying anything requiring hands up/down the stairs
  • Entering a shower
  • Standing in a shower
  • Showering
  • Putting on socks
  • Wiping (who’s going to help you now?)

So, in a sense, by losing a knee, you lose three limbs. Very interesting experience indeed.

People will help you

And you should accept their offers. No more self reliance and I-can-do-everything attitude, because now you genuinely need the help. Maybe you need a stool. Maybe you need a pillow. Maybe you cannot find your crutches. Maybe you need help getting into a car. Remember you are no longer the person you were, but you are not useless just yet. Accept the help.

People around you will suddenly be more aware of accessibility

Which is a good thing. Not until now had I thought those automatic door buttons being extremely slow being brilliant design. If they opened any faster, you wouldn’t be able to dodge the swinging doors.

They will mistake crutches for canes

Sometimes you simply cannot walk with just one crutch, as is the case when the bad leg cannot bear any load.
Canes are for people with two functional (but weak) legs. If you take a crutch from me, I can only hop on my good leg.

You are not alone; there are many people just like you

And because of that, outside your own place of dwelling, it is actually not that bad. Any form of official building would have a code that requires a level of accessibility to be taken into account. You legitimately qualify for the handicap (“people who identify with disability”, according to Ryerson’s Ted Rogers School of Management) washrooms now. You can use the lift/elevator for one single floor, without being perceived as a douche. I have not tried this yet, but shopping malls have scooters that are designed for people like us/you, and/or the morbidly obese.

Healing takes a long time

And you should not rush it. Trying to bend the knee will make it worse (in my case, anyway). Trying to stand on the bad leg will also make it worse, often permanently. There is no need to rush it recovery, because it is happening as quickly as you let it.

You can use the crutch like a stick

Unlike normal people using a crutch like a stick, you using the crutch like a stick is seen as an advantage; a clever idea. People with legs poking light switches from their seats is just being lazy.

Exercise whenever you can

Muscle loss will occur immediately after the injury. Consult with a physiotherapist to see what kinds of exercise you can do, and how often you can do them.

Don’t go to work

What are you, insane? Going to work not only increases the chances of you hurting yourself, you will also delay healing by inducing additional stress (pointed out by a mate who goes by “YS”.) If you can work from home, then you don’t need to move. Win. If you cannot work from home, then the company cannot fire you for not working (but they can do so for other reasons). Also win.

“It only gets better from here” is not true

Just because an injury is acute, but it doesn’t mean things will only get better. For example, an injury to the knee significantly increases the chance of that same knee developing arthritis, especially so for the case if you try to use the bad knee before it fully recovers. If you don’t take good care of yourself, no one will.

Roommates: Red Flags

The community list of red flags when assessing a roommate/potential roommate is as follows:

  • Insists on putting their name on their own food in the fridge, while eating all unlabelled food when no one else is doing it.
  • Has a mother move in for them. Chances are, this roommate will not be doing their own chores.
  • Expects the place to be clean, yet, does not clean anything.
  • Has a smell that lingers wherever they go.
  • Was forced to move out by their former roommates.
  • Is close to you. Once they move in, their flaws magnify. The closer you were, the more flaws you’ll find. Not a bad thing if they are nice though, since that magnifies positively as well.
  • Mentions how clean the place is before/upon move-in. Indicates different living/cleanliness standards.
  • Is a problem drunk. Sleep drunk is their own business.
  • Posts post-it notes in the house. Evidence of passive-aggression.
  • Always keeps the door open (sociopath), or always keeps the door closed (anti-social).
  • Leave dishes lying around.
  • For some reason, showers or poops with the door open, or showers and poops at the same time.
  • Uses metal utensils on non-stick pans (only madmen do this).
  • Has incredibly loud sex, but does not have the courtesy to invite you.
  • Drops weights on hardwood flooring.
  • Is alcoholic, and expects roommates carry them home.
  • Doesn’t have a job, and is not looking for one.
  • Has no sense of humour.
  • Distributes drugs outdoors as an occupation.
  • Has a Tumblr account.
  • Says, “I like to party occasionally.” If they have to say it, they know very well what occasionally means.
  • Is in debt.
  • Is a Republican. Even if your country of residence does not have a Republican party, this still applies.
  • Mentions to you, and makes sure everyone knows, that someone has a restraining order against them.
  • Has a degree in chemistry, and asks if anyone else is using the shed.
  • Smokes, or if you find any indoor ashtrays.
  • Mentions “I cook bad food”. That just means “I burn the stove a lot”.
  • Is the bad kind of racist. “Bad” is the difference between “haha Germans can’t make jokes” and “haha Germans should kill themselves”.

Easy Autism Test

Content warning: this article discusses nothing in particular.

Self diagnosis of autism is not reliable; we should not be diagnosing ourselves with anything. Self-diagnosis has high false negative and false positive rates, properties not desirable in an experiment.

You should go to a psychologist for such diagnoses. All, though, people are increasingly diagnosed with autism—which is no surprise, considering almost everyone has a disorder of some kind. To diagnose yourself with autism, without the disadvantages of self-diagnosis, single blind tests should be conducted, using participants who do not know that the test is being conducted, and who/m know you relatively well.

Two of the three following methods require a non-reproducible setting, where you are already holding a conversation with your participants. The procedures cannot be reproduced, on par with other modern psychology experimental standards.

Method 1: De Facto Association

Procedure

Hold a conversation with your participant. Without revealing your intention, state that

Since as you know I am autistic, I [do something completely normal, such as drawing].

Results

If the participant thinks that you are autistic, they will not have reacted to such a statement.
On the other hand, should the participant find you a healthy, normal human being, you will be asked why you said that.

Method 2: Proof by Negation

The second method, derived from the million-dollar social experiment, uses two preposterous premises in order to reveal the one that is more ridiculous, depending on setting.

Procedure

Hold a conversation with your participant. Without revealing your intention, state that

Since I am autistic and [the opposite of your sexual orientation], I [do something completely normal, such as drawing].

Results

If the participant thinks that you are autistic, their response will not contain the autism component, such as “I didn’t know you are [straight]’”.
On the other hand, should the participant find you a healthy, normal human being, you will be responded with “What?”.

Method 3: Scientific Method

The third, and perhaps the easiest method, but with by far the highest false negative rates, is by directly stating your hypothesis to your participant:

[Participant’s name], I have autism.

If you do that, you have autism.

Alternatively, just write an article like this one. Then, when you finish writing, you will know for sure that you have autism.

Introduction to HTML5 and Accessibility

The Importance of Accessibility

I’ll save myself some time writing up things that others that already have:

Accessible Web pages benefit everyone. The more accessible your content is, the more user-friendly it will be to all people, whether reading the page on a large screen or on a cell phone, or hearing the page read aloud by a screen-reader.

Principles of Accessibility

At the top are four principles that provide the foundation for Web accessibility:

  • Perceivable: the information must be available to at least one of the user’s senses.
  • Operable: the interface cannot require interaction that a user cannot perform.
  • Understandable: dumb it down.
  • Robust: design the content to be read by present and future (assistive) technologies.

Types of Accessibility to Cover

  • Visual disabilities (e.g., blindness, low vision and lack of color perception)
  • Hearing disabilities (e.g., hard of hearing, deafness)
  • People with physical disabilities (e.g., limited strength, reach or manipulation, tremor, lack of sensation)
  • People with speech disabilities
  • People with language, learning or cognitive disabilities (e.g., reading disabilities, thinking, remembering, sequencing disabilities)
  • Other disabilities (e.g., epilepsy, short stature)
  • Individuals with any combination of these disabling conditions (e.g., deaf-blindness)

Considerations for the Visually Impaired

People who are visually impaired often rely on screen-readers to read Web pages. Screen-readers translate Web pages into plain text, which is either read aloud by a voice synthesizer or translated by a Braille device…

Optimising for Screen Readers

In general,

  • Avoid “click here” links or vague link names.
  • Use semantic headings (H1, H2, etc.).
    “The H1 heading is in the banner”
    “The H2 heading is the title of the article”
    “So, in the body of your article, use H3 through H6 for your section heads.”
  • Use punctuation.
  • Keep words and layouts simple; only include words that need to be read aloud.
  • Images must have an alt attribute that makes sense.

Low Vision and Colour-Blindness

  • Ensure page can be zoomed, even on a mobile device.
    • WCAG requires to be > resized without assistive technology up to 200 percent in a way that does not require the user to scroll horizontally to read a line of text on a full-screen window.
  • Text and images must have sufficient contrast.
    “WCAG 2.0 Level AAA requires a contrast ratio of 7:1 for normal text and 4.5:1 for large text.”
    • BUT WCAG advises “using a light pastel background rather than a white background behind black text to create sufficient but not extreme contrast”
  • Empty text fields should not have the same colour as its parent.
  • Don’t rely on color alone to convey information.

Considerations for the Hearing Impaired

Some people with auditory disabilities can hear sounds but sometimes not sufficiently to understand all speech, especially when there is background noise. This includes people using hearing aids or other approaches to improve the sound.

  • Use video captions. Use <caption> tags.
  • Allow volume to be adjusted for videos.
  • Make as few content dependent on hearing as possible.
  • Provide sign language videos for audio-only files.

Considerations for the Physically Impaired

Physical impairment doesn’t just mean not having arms.

Physical disabilities include weakness, involuntary movements, tremors, lack of coordination, paralysis, limitations of sensation, joint problems (such as arthritis), …

  • Provide keyboard shortcuts.
    • Keybaord shortcuts cannot be combinations that require timing.
  • Ensure tab index is proper, and that all interactive elements can be focused.
  • MUST NOT impose a time limit to fill out a form or click a button.
  • MUST NOT rely on hover or complex navigation mechanisms.

Considerations for Speech Impairment

  • Avoid microphones.
  • Offer text input for situations where a microphone is necessary.

Considerations for Cognitive Impairment

… can impact how well people hear, move, see, speak, and understand information, …, not necessarily intelligence…

So it doesn’t just mean dumbing down the information. The problems that you can
eliminate mainly concern complex operations that are simply unnecessary.

  • Avoid complex navigation mechanisms.
  • Avoid long sentences that require the reader to keep track of its contents.
    Use this tool to check your language comprehension.
  • MUST NOT make objects blink, flicker, or move too quickly.
  • MUST NOT play background audio.
  • Offer stop buttons for music and videos.
  • To achieve WCAG 2.0 AAA compliance:
    • An expired session must be resumed completely after the user reauthenticates.
    • Breadcrumbs will be required if there isn’t a sitemap.

When and How to Use Which Tag

<*>

  • Use readable fonts. Check with the Colour contrast checker to make sure text is readable.
  • Text sizes must be in either %, em, or one of the named sizes.
    • line-height must also be defined with a percentage between 150% and 200%.
  • Any element that has a role must have an role attribute.
  • You should never define these abstract roles on any element:
    • command
    • composite
    • input
    • landmark
    • range
    • roletype
    • section
    • sectionhead
    • select
    • structure
    • widget
    • window
  • Text should not be justify aligned.
  • Text should not be center aligned either.
  • Columns should have limited width.

<a>

  • Links must have a :focus style that is different from its normal style.
  • Links should always be underlined by default.
  • Links must define role="link", except when the link doesn’t navigate anywhere, in which case its role is button instead.

<abbr>

  • If a <dfn> defines an acronym or an abbreviation, this needs to be in <dfn>.
  • <acronym> is non-standard so there is no debate on which one to use.

<b>

  • MUST NOT use <b>.
    Consider using <strong>, or if the style has no semantic meaning, use a bold CSS style instead.

<body>

  • [Always declare a background-color.
    Mostly because it is near-free to declare a background colour for no drawbacks.

<div>

<div> is the single most abused element in the HTML spec, primarily because
it has no meaning.

  • <div> should contain lang and title if it is not enclosed by one that does.

<dfn>

  • <dfn> should always contain an ID for later reference.

<form> series: <button>, <fieldset>, <input>, <label>, <legend>, <select>, <textarea>, …

  • <form> must have a role, e.g. <form role="search">.
  • For interactive elements, aria-disabled must be defined, and either true or false.

  • If an element is only visible after some user action, authors must set the aria-hidden attribute to true.

  • Besides invalid and ng-invalid, aria-invalid is also required, and be either true or false.
  • Elements that have readonly defined should also define aria-readonly.
  • <input type="text"> also has an role="textbox".
  • Optional fields should be hidden.
  • To get WCAG 2.0 AAA, submissions must be reversible, and have a mechanism to confirm.
  • Forms should not have a reset button.
    “The worst problem about Reset is that users click the button by mistake when they wanted to click Submit . Bang — all your work is gone!”
    • “Exception: Use Reset for Repeated Form-Filling” (RTFA)

Sliders

  • Sliders must define all these attributes at once:

<heading>, <header>

  • There is no <heading>.
  • Headings (<h1>, <h2>, <h3>, <h4>, <h5>, <h6>) must come in that order.
  • <header> must be used to wrap the site’s primary <nav>.

<hgroup>

  • You MUST NOT use <hgroup>.

<html>

  • <html> requires a lang attribute (it is not always en, by the way)

<i>

  • Unless the tag encloses a term in a foreign langage, MUST NOT use <i>.
    Consider using <em>, or if the style has no semantic meaning, use an italic (or cursive) CSS style instead.
  • Language terms must have a lang attribute, e.g. <i lang="fr">Merde</i>.
  • Language terms that are in the page’s language should consider <dfn> instead.

Font awesome

Using <i> for fontawesome icons is in fact invalid WCAG 2.0 code.
Simply use <span> in place of <i>.

<img>

  • <img> is self-closing in HTML.
  • <img> tags require a unique and meaningful alt attribute.

<label>

  • In addition to a matching <label>, all form elements (except <label>, of course) must define either an aria-label or aria-labelledby attribute.
  • If the label text is visible on screen, authors SHOULD use aria-labelledby and SHOULD NOT use aria-label.
    • And if the label is long, use aria-describedby instead.

<link> and <meta>

All pages should contain these meta tags for meaningful accessibility and SEO.
More info about meta tags

Optionally, pages can have these tags where applicable:

Open graph tags

While OG tags MUST NOT do anything to aid in accessibility, these commonly-used
OG tags (not mentioned above) should be added anyway:

<nav>

  • All elements in a navbar must be enclosed in a <nav>, despite that link saying it is optional.
  • <nav> must have role="navigation":
    • First level <ul>s must have role="menubar"
    • Second level <ul>s must have role="menu"
    • Popups must have role="presentation", while the trigger must have aria-haspopup="true".

<option>

  • aria-selected and aria-checked are optional attributes that override focus state.

<p>

  • Semantically, only when it is a paragraph.
    Do not use <p> for padding or spacing.

<script>

  • Never write inline scripts.
  • /* <![CDATA[ */ and /* ]]> */ are necessarily only when the doctype is XHTML.

<select>

  • Is there an <optgroup> where applicable?

<span>

  • Just because you can use spans for everything, it doesn’t mean you should.
    Prefer semantic tags over <span> where possible.

Tooltips

A tooltip whose role is tooltip should be loaded before any <input>
uses that tooltip in its aria-describedby attribute.

<strike>

  • Never use it.

<textarea>

  • Text areas have an role="textbox".
  • aria-multiline must be true.

<u>

  • You MUST NOT use <u>. Use text-decoration: underline instead.
