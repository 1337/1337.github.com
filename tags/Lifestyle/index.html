Why Do You Make More Friends in High School?

Dunbar’s number proposes that the average person can hold relationships with at most 150 other people, with 5 intimate family members, 15 close friends, 50 other kinds of friends, and 150 acquaintances being the most-commonly quoted. So let’s suppose Robin Dunbar said the average person has 50 friends. Which I don’t. (me irl)

With that figure, I propose a “Double Dunbar factor”, Q, where the requirement for making a new friend goes by this equation:

$$
\sqrt{Q} = max(\frac{50}{n}, 1)
$$

This means, if you were in a group of n=1 person, you are capable of making one friend (assuming your interests align and all that). If you were in a group of n=150 people, you are capable of making at most (Q * 150 = 50) friends.

With that said, just because you are able to make a friend, it does not automatically mean the other person considers you a friend. We need to double the Dunbar:

$$
Q = max(\frac{50}{n}, 1) \cdot max(\frac{50}{n}, 1)
$$

In a 30-person classroom, the equation reduces to

$$
Q = 1
$$

Which means you can make at least one friend in that class, and

$$
30Q = 30 \cdot 1 = 30
$$

However, if you were in a 300-person lecture hall:

$$
Q = max(\frac{50}{300}, 1) \cdot max(\frac{50}{300}, 1) = \frac{1}{6} \cdot \frac{1}{6} = \frac{1}{36}
$$

Which means, for any given person in that room, your likelihood of being friends with each other is 3%. Multiplying the probability with the number of people in that lecture hall, you get:

$$
300Q = \frac{300}{36} = 8.\dot3
$$

This means (if everyone in your Arts lecture is social and like each other) you should be able to make 8 friends in your class.

If you believe yourself (and others) to be less social than ideal, you can redo all the calculations with the Dunbar number being 10 (which means you assume people have 10 friends instead of 50).

$$
Q = max(\frac{10}{n}, 1) \cdot max(\frac{10}{n}, 1)
$$

In realistic terms, if you want to make friends at work (where you have, say, 20 people on your team), you will likely find:

$$
\frac{10}{20} \cdot \frac{10}{20} \cdot 20 = 5
$$

Five friends. On the other hand, if you have 50 people on your team, you may be surprised to make fewer friends than before.

$$
\frac{10}{50} \cdot \frac{10}{50} \cdot 50 = 2
$$

People don’t work in fixed teams though, and people definitely don’t work on the same stuff, so… what I am going to say is, if friendship were so easy to explain, I would have made a few more friends by now.

How to Beat an Escape Room

  1. Think simple: the average, dumb person can do this.
  2. Search everywhere: look under tables and rugs, except places that are clearly not part of the game, such as inside fire extinguishers and behind electrical sockets.
  3. Srganise your clues: so you know what’s missing, as well as keeping used clues in its own pile.
  4. Focus on what’s stopping you from moving forward: working backward from what you need to unblock it.
  5. Identify team roles: most important one being a project manager who identifies what needs to be done.
  6. Know your lock types: Combo locks need numbers (and sometimes letters). Padlocks need keys. If you need to open a padlock, look for keys.
  7. Identify code types: like morse code and braille.
  8. Be good at identifying hidden clues
  9. Look for patterns
  10. Listen to the guide who brought you into the room: They give you hints.

How to Talk to People

炸 [zhá]

I make my living talking to people. Nobel Prize winners, truck drivers, billionaires, kindergarten teachers, heads of state, plumbers… I talk to people that I like; I talk to people that I don’t like; I talked to some people that I disagree with deeply on a personal level, but I still have a great conversation with them.

Don’t multitask.

Be in that moment. Don’t think about anything else. Remove your conversational filters and talk, and share (aka “give and take”). If you think about your filters, you stop paying attention.

There is no reason to learn how to show you’re paying attention if you are in fact paying attention.

After “that moment”

  • Think about what happened and what was discussed.
  • Assess your body language that you used at the time.
  • Assess your body language that the other people used at the time.

Don’t pontificate. (3.1)

v. To express opinions or judgments in a dogmatic way.

Set your opinions aside. Enter every conversation assuming you have something to learn.

This also means: be a listener.

Use open-ended questions.

Don’t ask questions that can be answered with “yes” or “no”. If you don’t know how to do that, ask questions that start with “who”, “what”, “when”, “where”, or “why”, all of which guarantee an answer that is not “yes” or “no”.

Go with the flow.

Conversation ideas can come and go. You might think of a clever thing to ask two minutes before the person stopped talking, at which point it is no longer appropriate to ask it. Let them come and let them go.

If you don’t know, say that you don’t know.

Don’t claim to be an expert on anything.

Don’t equate your experience with theirs. (3.2)

It is never the same. All experiences are individual. Don’t take the moment to share your experience.

But remember don’t be afraid to be vulnerable. Everyone is flawed in some way. Don’t be afraid to show it.

Try not to repeat yourself.

It’s condescending.

Stay out of the weeds.

No one cares about the details (weeds). Leave them out.

LISTEN. (2.4)

If your mouth is open, you’re not learning. - Claims to be said by a buddha
He who talks more is sooner exhausted. - Lao Tzu

People love to talk about the things that matter to them the most. (2.4) Listen to them. Talk to them about those things. (2.5)
You have to listen with the intent to understand, not just the intent to answer.

Be brief.

Be genuinely interested in other people. Everyone has some hidden, amazing things about them. Keep your mouth shut for as long as possible. (2.1)

Smile. (2.2)

Fake it ‘til you make it.

Call people by their names. (2.3)

People love their own names.

If all else fails

Stick to the weather (excluding “global warming is a hoax”) and your health (excluding topics about vaccines).

Living in an Aprtment: Pros and Cons

My landlord is letting his place go on sale. This also means my eviction is in order (and, given the reason, has a very generous notice required by law). It is time to revisit what it was like to live in an apartment, and what people with similar experiences gained/missed.

Pros

  1. Stairs optional.
  2. Parking is usually underground. With locks.
  3. Gated entry guaranteed.
  4. Usually more to see out of windows (unless you rent the basement)
  5. Laundry en suite or in building is almost guaranteed.
  6. There may be other amenities like a fitness room, grocery store, and even a pool. Excellent for people wanting/waiting to die here.
  7. The power will probably never go out. If the power goes out, poor old grannies will be trapped, and they probably won’t let that happen.
  8. No snow. No grass.

Cons

  1. You have to share the lift. And if anyone comes in, you have to pretend you know them and smile.
  2. Possible noise from upstairs or downstairs.
  3. Possible noise from the building itself (if central air)
  4. Really troublesome to move in and out.
  5. It takes minutes rather than seconds to leave the building. Add more time is driving your car out of that underground parking.
  6. Mailboxes are small, and no one leaves your packages at your door. Because they can’t get to your door.
  7. Setting off the smoke alarm might cause the entire building to evacuate.
  8. No space for car washes.
  9. Much higher likelihood of someone sniffing your packets and hacking your Wi-Fis.
  10. Way more diseases.
  11. No more sound of raindrops from the roof.
