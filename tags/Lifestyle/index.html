How to Talk to People

炸 [zhá]

I make my living talking to people. Nobel Prize winners, truck drivers, billionaires, kindergarten teachers, heads of state, plumbers… I talk to people that I like; I talk to people that I don’t like; I talked to some people that I disagree with deeply on a personal level, but I still have a great conversation with them.

Don’t multitask.

Be in that moment. Don’t think about anything else. Remove your conversational filters and talk, and share (aka “give and take”). If you think about your filters, you stop paying attention.

There is no reason to learn how to show you’re paying attention if you are in fact paying attention.

After “that moment”

  • Think about what happened and what was discussed.
  • Assess your body language that you used at the time.
  • Assess your body language that the other people used at the time.

Don’t pontificate.

v. To express opinions or judgments in a dogmatic way.

Set your opinions aside. Enter every conversation assuming you have something to learn.

This also means: be a listener.

Use open-ended questions.

Don’t ask questions that can be answered with “yes” or “no”. If you don’t know how to do that, ask questions that start with “who”, “what”, “when”, “where”, or “why”, all of which guarantee an answer that is not “yes” or “no”.

Go with the flow.

Conversation ideas can come and go. You might think of a clever thing to ask two minutes before the person stopped talking, at which point it is no longer appropriate to ask it. Let them come and let them go.

If you don’t know, say that you don’t know.

Don’t claim to be an expert on anything.

Don’t equate your experience with theirs.

It is never the same. All experiences are individual. Don’t take the moment to share your experience.

But remember don’t be afraid to be vulnerable. Everyone is flawed in some way. Don’t be afraid to show it.

Try not to repeat yourself.

It’s condescending.

Stay out of the weeds.

No one cares about the details (weeds). Leave them out.

LISTEN.

If your mouth is open, you’re not learning. - Claims to be said by a buddha

You have to listen with the intent to understand, not just the intent to answer.

Be brief.

Be interested in other people. Everyone has some hidden, amazing things about them. Keep your mouth shut for as long as possible.

If all else fails

Stick to the weather (excluding “global warming is a hoax”) and your health (excluding topics about vaccines).

Living in an Aprtment: Pros and Cons

My landlord is letting his place go on sale. This also means my eviction is in order (and, given the reason, has a very generous notice required by law). It is time to revisit what it was like to live in an apartment, and what people with similar experiences gained/missed.

Pros

  1. Stairs optional.
  2. Parking is usually underground. With locks.
  3. Gated entry guaranteed.
  4. Usually more to see out of windows (unless you rent the basement)
  5. Laundry en suite or in building is almost guaranteed.
  6. There may be other amenities like a fitness room, grocery store, and even a pool. Excellent for people wanting/waiting to die here.
  7. The power will probably never go out. If the power goes out, poor old grannies will be trapped, and they probably won’t let that happen.
  8. No snow. No grass.

Cons

  1. You have to share the lift. And if anyone comes in, you have to pretend you know them and smile.
  2. Possible noise from upstairs or downstairs.
  3. Possible noise from the building itself (if central air)
  4. Really troublesome to move in and out.
  5. It takes minutes rather than seconds to leave the building. Add more time is driving your car out of that underground parking.
  6. Mailboxes are small, and no one leaves your packages at your door. Because they can’t get to your door.
  7. Setting off the smoke alarm might cause the entire building to evacuate.
  8. No space for car washes.
  9. Much higher likelihood of someone sniffing your packets and hacking your Wi-Fis.
  10. Way more diseases.
  11. No more sound of raindrops from the roof.

Living Alone: Pros and Cons

My landlord is letting his place go on sale. This also means my eviction is in order (and, given the reason, has a very generous notice required by law). It is time to revisit what it was like to live alone, and what people with similar experiences gained/missed.

Pros

  1. No one will ever touch your food.
  2. The whole fridge is yours.
  3. You can talk to yourself without anyone else thinking you are crazy.
  4. Cleaning schedule is “whenever”.
  5. No quiet hours (except otherwise specified by building).
  6. No one touches your electrical appliances (if any), and they are always available to use. Wash your clothes at 3am if you want.
  7. You can sing in the shower as poorly as your heart desires.
  8. You are almost guaranteed more space per capita.
  9. The door is where you put your shoes.
  10. Thermostat is set by you. Air conditioning is set by you.
  11. Your place may be eligible for 100% of the parking spots (which is usually one).
  12. Can poop with door open. The stench is always your very own.
  13. The bathroom is always free. Toilet paper lasts forever.
  14. Everything is always where you left it.
  15. You can have friends over any time.
  16. You can be completely naked, anywhere, anytime. You can also do anything while naked, anywhere, anytime.
  17. No one uses your soap. You can be sure your razor has not touched the testes of any man (or any other man).
  18. All the Internet bandwidth belongs to you. Also, no one will hack you from the inside.
  19. The sink can be used, perpetually, for soaking dishes that really needed soaking, rather than being called a lazy bastard every other hour.
  20. You can keep various pets and plants with only your rental agreement and/or the law to worry about (where applicable).
  21. You can choose where to sleep. You can sleep on the couch, the laundry room, or above the toilet bowl for all we care.
  22. You can fill the entire house/apartment with cooking smoke and only you will be shamed of what you did.

Cons

  1. Appliances might not come as standard. May need to do your laundry elsewhere.
  2. No noise made by other humans. Can be lonely.
  3. You pay all bills not covered by the lease. This is less likely when living with others.
  4. More expensive rent per capita. Sometimes also higher per unit area.
  5. If anything squeaks and leaks or falls apart, you know you are the only person who could have broken it.
  6. You are almost certainly guaranteed not to have a dishwasher. You might not even have a proper stove.
  7. If anything is dirty, you are cleaning it. Though, you know you were the one dirtying it in the first place.
  8. With no one to gauge your QOL, it is completely possible that you will become a social degenerate.
  9. Inability to share 4L bags of milk.
  10. You never carpool (except if you drive for Uber, maybe).
  11. You never share the cost of your car.
  12. No one will share their Netflix account with you.
  13. If you have a pet, you and you alone must walk it.
  14. You alone will defend the home intruder.
  15. You can invite friends over only if you have the space. You might not have the space.
  16. Losing keys can be a very costly ordeal.
  17. No one to practice interviews with. Or talk to, on a daily basis.
  18. If you torrent anything, they will know it is you.
  19. Too much free space might pressure you to get all sorts of furniture you never needed.
  20. You pay your own home insurance.
  21. Snow? You are shovelling (where applicable). Grass? You might get away with paying a boy to cut it for you, but that also costs money.
  22. It is less likely that you will find someone willing to help you move.
  23. If you are a stroke or heart attack, you die.
